Before any strategy: you are building something that almost no one has seen before. A robot with 84 solenoids that physically presses keys on a real keyboard, controlled by AI. The hook writes itself: *"I'm building a robot so AI can type on a real keyboard."*

People will immediately ask: "Why not just use software?" That question IS your content engine. You answer it over 12 weeks.

## Content Pillars

Every post you make should fit into one of these five buckets:

### 1. The Milestone Moment
The first solenoid fires. The first letter types. The first word. The first sentence. The first time the AI completes a task end-to-end. These are your biggest posts. Film them properly. Post them immediately. Add a number: "Day 14: First keystroke."

### 2. The Explainer
"Why does this need 84 solenoids?" "How does a solenoid even work?" "Why is 19mm pitch important?" Short, fast, educational. These get saved and shared. They build authority.

### 3. The Struggle
Wrong parts ordered. Solenoid fires sideways. CAD doesn't fit. PSU current math is wrong. These are your most *relatable* posts and often outperform the wins. Be honest. The M2 vs M2.5 screw discovery? That's a video. "I ordered 168 wrong screws" is a great hook.

### 4. The Unboxing / Arrival
Parts arriving from AliExpress. The 100-solenoid haul. The prototype kit. These are satisfying and cheap to film — just set up your phone pointed at your desk. The solenoids clicking in the box will sound incredible.

### 5. The Big Question
Polls, debates, design choices. "Should the side rails run front-to-back or left-to-right?" "What should the robot type first?" Community involvement creates investment. They'll follow to see if their answer wins.

## Platform Strategy

### TikTok & Instagram Reels & YouTube Shorts
*(Same video, posted on all three)*

**Format:** Vertical 9:16. 30–60 seconds. Captions ON (always).
**Structure:**
- **0–2 sec:** Visual hook. Show the most impressive thing first — solenoid matrix firing, robot typing, parts spread across a table. NO intro, NO "hey guys."
- **2–10 sec:** Stakes. Why should anyone care? One sentence.
- **10–45 sec:** The story/explanation/struggle.
- **45–60 sec:** Payoff or cliffhanger. Either the satisfying result OR "next video I'll find out if this works."

**Posting cadence:** 2x per week. Tuesday and Friday are your best days to post. Aim for 6–9pm local time.

**Audio:** Use trending sounds under build content (check TikTok's trending audio weekly). For milestone moments, let the real sound lead — 84 solenoids firing is better than any trending audio.

## The Audio Advantage

This is your secret weapon and almost no one talks about it: **the sound of your robot will drive engagement.**

84 solenoids firing in sequence sounds like a typewriter from the future. A single keystroke sounds like a satisfying mechanical click. When your robot types its first word, the audio is shareable on its own.

**Tactical use:**
- Never mute the real sound in build videos unless you need to
- For at least one video, strip all music and let the solenoid clicks be the only audio
- The "ASMR robot typing" angle is a real sub-niche — consider one dedicated clip
- When the full matrix fires, record it with your phone mic AND a directional mic if you have one

## What Viral Build Creators Do Differently

The accounts that broke out — Stuff Made Here, Michael Reeves, Simone Giertz, William Osman — share these traits:

**They show the failure honestly.** The robot types the wrong key. The solenoid misfires. The CAD doesn't fit. Showing failure is not weakness — it's proof you're actually building something hard, and it makes the eventual success feel earned.

**They are specific.** Not "I built a robot." "I built a robot with 84 solenoids, each one wired to a different key, that fires in 20 milliseconds, controlled by an AI running on my laptop." Specificity = credibility = shareability.

**They talk to the audience like they're smart.** Don't oversimplify. Say "solenoid" not "magnet thing." Explain what a 5N force means. People will google the rest — and that means they're invested.

**They have a recurring bit or catchphrase.** Simone had "shitty robots." Reeves had "I built a robot that [does something terrible]." For you: every video ends with the current count. "84 keys down. 0 typed. Next week that changes." Or a consistent frame: "Week X of building the world's most unnecessary keyboard."

**They post consistently.** Viral moments need an audience to land. The audience comes from posting consistently even when nothing viral is happening. A "Week 3: Everything is still in boxes" video still builds your following.

## Starting From Zero: The First 30 Days

The hardest part is the first 30 days with no audience. Here's the play:

**Week 1:** Post the concept video. One 60-second video: what you're building, why, what the finished thing will do. Don't have footage yet? Film yourself at your desk explaining it. Show the spreadsheet. Show the parts you've ordered. The ambition alone is compelling.

**Week 2:** Post an explainer. "Why does an AI need to physically type?" This is the question everyone will have. Answer it. This video will get shared by AI-curious people who aren't even in the maker community.

**Week 3:** Post the first unboxing. Solenoids arriving. Film the entire unbox in one continuous shot. The pile of 100 solenoids on your desk is a striking image.

**Throughout:** Comment on 10–20 posts per day from accounts in your space on X. Not spam replies — actual thoughts. This is how you get initial followers before you have a big post.

## What NOT to Do

- Don't wait until it's finished to start posting. The journey IS the content.
- Don't use copyrighted music on TikTok/Reels (use the in-app music library or royalty-free sources like Uppbeat or Pixabay)
- Don't post without captions. 85% of videos on TikTok/Reels are watched on mute.
- Don't explain the joke. If something is funny or satisfying, let it land. Cut before it gets boring.
- Don't post the same video on the same day to all platforms. Stagger by 1–2 days. The algorithm rewards unique content.
- Don't obsess over production value early. Your phone + decent light + interesting subject matter beats professional gear + boring subject. Get the tripod and the ring light from Amazon ($30 total). That's enough.
